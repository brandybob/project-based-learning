WHAT IS SWIMMING?

Swimming is the act of self-propulsion through water using coordinated arm and leg movements. It serves as a vital life-saving skill, a competitive sport, and a low-impact form of exercise. By utilizing the body's natural buoyancy, swimmers navigate aquatic environments through four primary competitive strokes: freestyle, backstroke, breaststroke, and butterfly. Historically practiced for millennia, modern organized swimming emerged in the 19th century and has been a staple of the Olympic Games since 1896. Beyond safety and recreation, it offers a full-body workout that enhances cardiovascular health, builds muscle strength, and reduces stress.

BENEFITS OF SWIMMING

  1. Full-Body Workout: It engages almost every major muscle group, including your core, back, shoulders, arms, and legs.
  2. Heart and Lung Health: Regular swimming strengthens the heart muscle and improves lung capacity, which can lower blood pressure and reduce the risk of heart disease and stroke.
  3. Weight Management: It is an efficient calorie burner; a 30-minute moderate swim can burn up to 250 to 350 calories.
  4. Joint-Friendly Exercise: The buoyancy of water supports up to 90% of your body weight, making it ideal for those with arthritis, injuries, or during pregnancy.

RISKS OF SWIMMING

Although there are some risks when it comes to swimming, often there are few fatalities and some risks are due to ignorance and can be reduced close to nil if only strict rules are adhered to. Some of the risk include:

  • Drowning
  • Waterborne Illnesses & Infections
  • Physical Injuries (Acute & Overuse)
  • Environmental Hazards (Open Water)
  • Temperature Extremes

CONTIGENCIES

There are some contigencies kept in place to curb the risks that are posed by swimming. These contigencies reduce the number of fatalities to a close zero point.The include:

  • Constant Supervision ("Water Watcher")
  • Physical Barriers and Access Control
  • Survival Skills and Water Competency
  • Proper Safety Equipment
  • Emergency Preparedness and Medical Readiness
